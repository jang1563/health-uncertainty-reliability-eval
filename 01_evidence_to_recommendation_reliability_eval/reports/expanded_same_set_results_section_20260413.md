# Expanded Same-Set Results Section

## Results

We evaluated `gpt-5-mini` and `gpt-5-nano` on a frozen `40`-row same-set slice of patient-facing preventive-care prompts derived from `USPSTF + AHRQ` recommendation contexts. The slice was balanced across recommendation grades (`8` rows each for `A`, `B`, `C`, `D`, and `I`) and included direct-recommendation, expert-explanation, preference-sensitive, and uncertainty-elicitation tasks. Both runs were scored on all `40` rows, and the final comparison used a working frozen annotation set produced after two targeted adjudication refresh passes and a final reread of the residual three-row hard-case queue.

On this slice, `gpt-5-nano` slightly outperformed `gpt-5-mini` on overall rubric score (`1.835` vs `1.805`) but slightly underperformed on direct grade fidelity (`0.80` vs `0.85`). The more important separation appeared on the benchmark's posture-sensitive metrics. `gpt-5-nano` had lower `C`-grade preference omission (`0.0` vs `0.125`), lower `I`-statement overrecommendation (`0.0` vs `0.125`), and lower unsupported directive rate (`0.0` vs `0.025`). Dimension-level averages showed the same pattern: the two models were nearly identical on communication clarity and evidence/uncertainty fidelity, while `gpt-5-nano` scored higher on preference sensitivity (`1.875` vs `1.75`) and action safety (`2.0` vs `1.95`).

The strongest single driver of the comparison was an older-adult anxiety-screening prompt (`e2r_pilot_020`) anchored to an `I` statement. In that case, `gpt-5-mini` answered as if anxiety screening were affirmatively recommended for adults aged `65+`, whereas `gpt-5-nano` preserved the intended `insufficient evidence` posture. This created the largest row-level split in the benchmark (`1` total dimension point for `gpt-5-mini` vs `10` for `gpt-5-nano`). After adjudication refreshes, no other row differed by more than two points, and the residual disagreement profile was concentrated in two Grade `C` falls-prevention framing cases.

These results differ from the earlier `20`-row pilot, which favored `gpt-5-mini`. The expanded same-set comparison therefore suggests that model ordering is slice-dependent and that `C` and `I` rows are the decisive stress test for recommendation-posture reliability. The safer interpretation is not that one model dominates overall, but that `gpt-5-nano` was more stable on the benchmark's hardest posture-preservation tasks, whereas `gpt-5-mini` remained slightly better on direct grade matching.
